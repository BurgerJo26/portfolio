01 — Overview

Voyc's customers weren't reaching value fast enough.

Voyc is a UK-based startup that uses AI to monitor 100% of call-center conversations for compliance breaches — a product that pays for itself the moment it catches a single mis-selling event. But customers weren't getting to that moment fast enough.

New clients were stuck in onboarding for up to six months before they could run a single script check, and our Customer Success team was drowning in repetitive setup calls. As the first product designer at Voyc, I led the end-to-end redesign of onboarding: from user research and journey mapping through to a shipped in-app self-service flow.

"We had customers on the verge of cancellation because they hadn't seen value yet. Every new client was burning Customer Success hours we didn't have."

02 — Problem

Onboarding was taking months, not minutes.

Every new Voyc customer had to go through up to five 45-minute onboarding calls, plus roughly five hours of manual setup support. For a product that sells itself on speed of insight, this was the wrong first impression — and it was putting real revenue at risk. Three things, specifically, were going wrong:

Manual onboarding bottlenecks

The setup process depended on call after call with our Customer Success team. Customers couldn't move forward without us, and we couldn't scale without fixing that dependency.

No in-app guidance

Help content lived in a hidden side-nav and had to be hunted for. When a user hit confusion — which happened often, given the complexity of compliance scripting — their only option was to open a support ticket.

No visibility of progress

Users had no idea how far into setup they were, what came next, or whether they were nearly done. The uncertainty bred support queries of its own: "Am I doing this right?"

Journey map of the original five-call onboarding flow
03 — Discovery

Six research moves to pinpoint the friction.

Before designing anything, I needed to know exactly where the journey was breaking down — not from assumption, but from evidence.

  • Sat in on live onboarding sessions to watch customers struggle in real time
  • Conducted user interviews with customers at different points in the setup journey
  • Analysed support tickets to find the repeat offenders
  • Ran stakeholder workshops with Customer Success, Sales, and Engineering
  • Affinity-mapped everything in FigJam to cluster the pain points
  • Facilitated a prioritisation session to agree what we'd tackle first

What emerged was that the pain wasn't one big problem — it was a fragmented chain of small frictions, each easy to fix alone but compounding into a six-month slog. The answer wasn't a better manual process. It was a self-service, in-app flow that aligned with how customers actually thought about getting started.

04 — Challenges

Two challenges shaped the redesign.

Challenge 01

Turning a service problem into a product

How might we move the work of onboarding from human-led calls into the product itself, without losing the trust and reassurance those calls provided?

Challenge 02

Making progress visible at every step

How might we give compliance teams a clear sense of "where am I, what's next, and how close am I to done?" — across a setup journey with dozens of small tasks?

Solution 01

An in-app onboarding checklist, visible from day one.

I placed a live progress dashboard on the homepage: a completion score, an ordered list of setup tasks with effort indicators, and a floating checklist that persisted across screens. Users now knew exactly what to do next, how much was left, and how much effort each step would take.

Onboarding checklist on the homepage — final implementation
Solution 02

Help content, embedded where users need it.

Contextual tooltips, inline setup guides, and a redesigned Help & Info Hub surfaced the right resources at the right moment. Help stopped being a destination and became a behaviour of the interface itself.

Embedded contextual help patterns
Solution 03

A validated service flow before any code shipped.

I built the new flow as a service-level journey first, then tested it live during manual onboarding sessions with the Learning Experience team. That let us refine the logic with real customers before a single engineer touched it — saving build cost and catching problems our in-app prototypes would have missed.

05 — Impact

From six months to 64 days.

The numbers matter because they represent real customers reaching real value faster — and a Customer Success team that finally had time for strategic work instead of setup calls.

↓70% Setup time reduced from 5 hours to 1.5 hours
↓40% Onboarding calls reduced from 5 to 3
64 days Time-to-value down from 1.5–6 months

Critically, the change stuck. The self-service flow scaled cleanly with new customer growth, and Customer Success was finally freed up to do the strategic work the product's growth needed.

06 — Lessons learned

What this project taught me.

01Onboarding is a service problem

If I'd approached this as "make the in-app flow better," we'd have shipped prettier screens and saved maybe a call or two. The 70% win came from treating onboarding as a cross-functional service — Customer Success, Learning Experience, Product, Engineering — with the UI as one layer of a bigger system.

02Discovery de-risks everything

Six research activities felt excessive at the start. It was, in hindsight, exactly the right amount. Every design decision downstream was defensible because we could point to specific evidence behind it. Prioritisation became faster, not slower.

03Validate before you ship

The move I'm most proud of wasn't a screen — it was running the new flow as a live manual script with real customers before we built anything. That turned engineering from a guessing game into execution.

04UX writing is product design

Half the work of this project lived in tooltip copy, label clarity, and effort tags. Polishing the words inside components turned out to be one of the highest-leverage moves I made.
