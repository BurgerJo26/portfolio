Case Study 02 Capitec Bank · Business Banking Oct 2020 — Oct 2022 UX Designer

Redesigning business banking flows to cut cognitive load and user errors.

How I helped rebuild Capitec's most trust-sensitive screens — beneficiary groups and multi-payments — from chaotic, confidence-eroding interfaces into focused journeys users could move through without fear.

Business banking portal — redesigned home dashboard
01 — Overview

The TL;DR

Capitec's legacy business banking portal had a clarity problem. Business users — finance officers, bookkeepers, owners of small-to-medium enterprises — were making large, irreversible financial decisions on an interface that overwhelmed them with choices, buried critical actions, and used patterns that contradicted themselves from one screen to the next.

I worked as part of a three-designer team on the portal's rebrand and rebuild, with a focus on two of its highest-stakes flows: beneficiary group creation and multi-payments. The goal wasn't to ship something prettier. It was to build trust at the exact moments users were most likely to make a costly mistake.

"Users were paying beneficiaries one by one because they didn't trust the bulk-payment feature enough to use it. Fear was eating the feature's entire value."

My role

UX and UI design, prototyping, user-testing coordination (with Ipsos), stakeholder communication, and taking ownership of dependencies beyond the immediate design problem — which, on a team this size and a project this cross-functional, was often where the real work happened.

02 — Problem

The old portal was overloading users at the worst moments.

User research with Ipsos surfaced a consistent pattern: users found the portal's navigation confusing, its iconography unclear, and its terminology inconsistent with how they thought about their own work. But the most damaging finding was specific to the flows I'd end up owning.

Users were choosing the slower path on purpose:

  • Paying beneficiaries one at a time instead of using the bulk feature, because the bulk feature scared them
  • Avoiding group payments entirely because the UI "didn't feel safe"
  • Relying on branch staff for tasks the portal was explicitly designed to let them self-serve

In regulated fintech, that's not a usability problem. It's a business one. A feature nobody uses is a feature that costs money to maintain and earns nothing back.

03 — The redesign, pt. 1

Beneficiary groups — fewer decisions, presented in order.

The original beneficiary group screen asked users to do everything at once: name the group, see current members, see all available beneficiaries, add members, remove members, save — on a single page where the same checkbox pattern meant different things depending on which section you were in.

We brainstormed alternatives in InVision Freehand with the other designers and settled on three specific changes:

Limit simultaneous actions

Only the actions relevant to the current task are visible at any moment. Adding beneficiaries and removing them became separate, clearly-labelled flows instead of two identical-looking checkboxes that did opposite things.

Introduce modal flows for the two core actions

"Add beneficiaries" and "Remove beneficiaries" each became their own focused modal with its own step sequence. Users could no longer accidentally be doing the wrong thing.

Standardise component behaviour

One checkbox pattern, one meaning. One add button, one flow. One remove affordance, one flow. The payoff here is less visible than a redesigned layout, but it's where trust actually lives: when components behave predictably, users stop second-guessing themselves.

04 — The redesign, pt. 2

Multi-payments — building trust through structure.

The multi-payment flow was the highest-stakes screen in the portal: mistakes here meant real money moving to the wrong account. And it was the flow users were most actively avoiding.

Two structural changes did most of the heavy lifting:

Steppers to break the task into phases

A three-step progression — Payment Options → Beneficiaries → Confirm — let users focus on one decision at a time. Error-checking also became easier: if something looked wrong at the confirm step, users could step back without losing context.

Table-format data with explicit, labelled actions

Every beneficiary row shows exactly what's being sent, from which account, with which reference — and the actions available are explicitly named ("Remove", "Edit") rather than hidden behind misleading icons. Nothing here is invisible or implicit.

"The redesign wasn't about making it prettier. It was about letting users do the right thing without a knot in their stomach."

05 — Reflection

What this project taught me.

Consistency is an invisible UX feature — until it isn't

The biggest wins on this project weren't new patterns. They were the same pattern applied everywhere. When every checkbox means the same thing, users stop spending cognitive energy on the interface and start spending it on their actual task. In regulated domains especially, that trade is everything.

Enterprise UX is mostly about owning the invisible work

Rebuilding a banking portal meant working with business analysts, system analysts, account managers, developers, and a rotating cast of stakeholders — sometimes without a product owner present. The design was only as good as the documentation, the handoffs, and the patience to re-ground the team around users every time priorities drifted.

User testing is the cheapest honesty a team can buy

Working with Ipsos meant I couldn't argue my way past a pattern users couldn't parse. It grounded conversations with stakeholders who wanted to move faster than the evidence warranted — and it earned trust, over time, for the design decisions that followed.
